### Common medication interactions with health metrics:

- **PPIs** (omeprazole, pantoprazole, dexilant): Reduce absorption of iron, B12, magnesium, calcium
- **Beta-blockers** (metoprolol, atenolol): Lower HR and HRV — your baselines will be lower than population norms
- **Stimulants** (adderall, methylphenidate): Raise HR, may suppress HRV
- **SSRIs/SNRIs**: Can affect HRV and sleep architecture
- **Bupropion**: May increase HR slightly
- **Thyroid medication**: Dose changes directly affect HR and HRV within 2-4 weeks
- **Pseudoephedrine/decongestants**: Raise HR, suppress HRV for 12-24 hours

## Personal Baselines

**These are the most important numbers in this file.** Population norms tell you
where the average person sits. Your baselines tell you where YOU sit when you're
feeling good and recovered.

### How to Establish Your Baselines

1. Pick a 14-28 day period where you're:
   - Sleeping reasonably well (7+ hours most nights)
   - Not sick or recovering from illness
   - Not traveling across time zones
   - Not under unusual stress
   - Exercising at your normal level
   - Not drinking alcohol heavily

2. Record the median (middle value) for each metric over that period.
   Your Apple Watch or health app can show you averages — use those.

3. Update your baselines every 3-6 months, or after a major life change
   (new medication, significant weight change, altitude change, new exercise routine).

### Altitude-Adjusted SpO2 Reference

If you live above 3,000 feet, your SpO2 will be lower than sea-level norms.
This is normal physiology, not pathology.

| Altitude | Expected Healthy SpO2 | Notes |
|----------|----------------------|-------|
| Sea level - 2,000 ft | 96-99% | Standard reference range |
| 2,000 - 4,000 ft | 95-98% | Minimal reduction |
| 4,000 - 6,000 ft | 93-97% | Noticeable reduction; don't panic at 94% |
| 6,000 - 8,000 ft | 92-96% | Significant reduction |
| 8,000 - 10,000 ft | 90-95% | Large reduction; erythropoietic demand increases |
| >10,000 ft | 88-94% | High altitude; iron demand substantially increased |

**Iron and altitude:** Higher altitudes increase iron demand through chronic
erythropoietic stimulation. If you live above 4,000 ft and have low ferritin,
this may limit your body's ability to adapt. Target ferritin ≥50 ng/mL at altitude.
